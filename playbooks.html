Playbook 01Influence

Adoption without a mandate

01
Situation

Adobe Fonts, the licensed type library built into Creative Cloud, had to ship across a dozen of those apps. Every one run by a team I couldn't direct, each with its own roadmap and priorities.

You don't mandate a platform onto teams you don't manage. You make integrating it their win, and you measure the number they respect.

  1. Make the integration the app team's win, not yours. Frame it as their roadmap goal, delivered by the shared component you design once.
  2. Start where you're wanted: flagship anchors first, then the smaller apps follow the proof.
  3. Standardize that component so each new app costs less to ship than the one before it.
  4. Measure usage, not installs: report unique users actually using the feature.
Result

One font platform now powers 12 flagship apps and 15M MAU, built entirely by persuading teams I didn't manage. The same play won Photoshop and Express commitments to ship generative text editing (AI text replacement that matches the original font and lighting) after it proved out in Firefly Boards.

Playbook 02Measurement

Proof before you ship

02
Situation

Font features shipped across a dozen Creative Cloud apps, and their business impact kept getting argued in executive rooms after the fact. Each result was reconstructed from whatever data could be found later.

A number is only as strong as where it came from. Provenance, not precision, is what lets a result survive an executive room.

Decision rule

If you can't read the result the day you ship, you launched blind. The launch numbers come from dashboards designed before launch.

  1. Define success metrics with the data team before launch: adoption, failed-search rate, missing-font events. Agree on what each one proves.
  2. Instrument the dashboards pre-launch so impact is read, not reconstructed.
  3. Tie each metric back to the original business-case claim: drop-off pattern → search failure → adoption. Every number traces to a promise you made.
Result

Instrumented from day one, the Adobe Express redesign (Adobe's freemium design app) read out at +186% free-to-paid conversion the day it shipped. Same play, run again on a “fix my typography” feature that reads a document and corrects its type automatically, concept to public beta.

Playbook 03Positioning

Two buyers, one capability

03
Situation

One font capability had to win two buyers at once: individual designers who wanted freedom, and enterprise teams who wanted control, without a generic message that served neither.

Same capability, two true positions. Never average two audiences into one generic message.

Audience A · Individuals

Led with creative freedom: use any font you already own.

→Bring Your Own Fonts

Audience B · Enterprise

Led with governance and control: manage fonts across the organization.

→Custom Fonts

Decision rule

One capability can carry two messages; it must never carry an average of them.

Result

Both shipped under their own name and reached millions on their own terms: individuals won on freedom, enterprise on control, neither position averaged into the other.
